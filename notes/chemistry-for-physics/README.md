# Chemistry

These notes are a living reference on general chemistry developed alongside my research in experimental physics. Their primary purpose is to consolidate the chemical concepts, language, and laboratory practices that frequently appear in modern materials science, nanophotonics, solid-state physics, and related disciplines.

Rather than replacing a comprehensive chemistry textbook, these notes aim to provide a concise, organized, and practical foundation that emphasizes conceptual understanding, scientific terminology, and quantitative problem solving. Whenever appropriate, examples are drawn from laboratory work and materials commonly encountered in experimental physics.

The collection is intended to grow over time, gradually becoming a comprehensive reference that spans the core topics of general chemistry while remaining focused on clarity and practical application.

---

## Planned contents

### 1. Chemical Language and Nomenclature

The vocabulary of chemistry: how compounds are represented, classified, and named.

Topics include:

- Elements and chemical symbols
- Chemical formulas
- Chemical species
- Inorganic nomenclature
- Organic nomenclature
- Common laboratory compounds and solvents

Representative examples:

- Sodium chloride
- Titanium dioxide
- Copper sulfate
- Silver nitrate
- Ethanol
- Acetone
- Benzene
- Dimethyl sulfoxide (DMSO)

---

### 2. Chemical Reactions and Stoichiometry

The quantitative language of chemical reactions.

Topics include:

- Chemical equations
- Conservation of mass
- Balancing reactions
- Stoichiometric coefficients
- Limiting reagents
- Reaction yield

Applications include laboratory calculations, reagent preparation, and quantitative analysis.

---

### 3. Atomic Structure and Chemical Bonding

An introduction to the electronic structure of matter and the origin of chemical bonds.

Topics include:

- Electronic structure
- Valence electrons
- Periodic trends
- Ionic bonding
- Covalent bonding
- Metallic bonding
- Coordinate bonding
- Hybridization
- Intermolecular forces

---

### 4. Molecular Geometry and Crystal Symmetry

An introduction to the geometry and symmetry of both molecules and crystalline solids.

Topics include:

- Lewis structures (review)
- VSEPR theory
- Molecular geometry
- Molecular polarity
- Symmetry operations
- Molecular point groups
- Crystal lattices
- Bravais lattices
- Crystal systems
- Crystal classes
- Space-group notation (introductory)

Representative examples include water, methane, benzene, sodium chloride, silicon, quartz, and perovskites.

---

### 5. Solutions and Concentrations

The quantitative chemistry most frequently encountered in the laboratory.

Topics include:

- Solutes and solvents
- Solubility
- Density
- Molarity
- Molality
- Mole fraction
- Mass and volume percentages
- Parts per million (ppm)
- Parts per billion (ppb)
- Dilutions
- Serial dilutions

Worked examples will focus on preparing solutions, converting concentration units, and solving realistic laboratory problems.

---

## Organization

Each chapter follows a common structure:

1. **Concepts** — Definitions and fundamental ideas.
2. **Physical intuition** — Qualitative explanations connecting concepts to observable behavior.
3. **Worked examples** — Step-by-step solved problems.
4. **Laboratory examples** — Practical situations encountered in experimental work.
5. **Exercises** — Problems ranging from conceptual questions to numerical calculations.

---

## Scope

These notes assume a basic familiarity with atomic structure and introductory chemistry. The emphasis is placed on developing a clear understanding of chemical language, quantitative reasoning, molecular structure, and laboratory practice, providing a solid foundation for further study in chemistry, materials science, and experimental physics.

As with the rest of this repository, these documents are living notes. They are continuously revised, expanded, and refined as my understanding evolves.
